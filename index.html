One case

BabyBeam — what a baby monitor costs once you refuse the account.

The problem

A baby monitor is either dedicated hardware you use for two years and then put in a drawer, or an app that asks you to create an account so your child's bedroom can pass through a company's servers. The second is most of the market. An app that puts a camera in a nursery and then explains why it needs an account has solved the parent's problem and quietly created a new one.

The call

Video and audio go directly between the two phones over WebRTC, end-to-end encrypted with DTLS-SRTP. Nothing is recorded and nothing is kept, because there is no server to keep it on. The only thing that ever leaves the pair is a small pairing handshake, which passes briefly through iCloud encrypted under a key derived from the pairing code — a key I don't hold and can't recover.

This still works when you're not home. The two phones find each other over the internet, not just over your own Wi-Fi. What's gone is the account, the subscription and the company sitting in the middle of the picture.

What it cost

  • A setup barrier Both phones have to be signed in to iCloud. Not the same Apple Account, but signed in. Every cloud competitor gets to skip that sentence in their onboarding.
  • Connecting became the user's problem A service that routes everything through its own servers always connects, because there's only ever one route. Two phones finding each other directly usually works, but when a mobile carrier's network gets in the way the fix is a TURN relay you add yourself under the gear icon. I moved a class of failure off my plate and onto a settings screen.
  • It reaches the marketing The pairing code is the security boundary, so it can't appear anywhere, including in my own App Store screenshots. Those had to be retaken with it blurred out.

Where it stands

Live and free to download. It's been in the store a few weeks, so the honest answer to “did it work” is that I don't know yet. The ratings line in the log above comes from the App Store and will say so when there's something to say.

On AI

AI writes most of the code in these apps. That isn't the interesting part; it stopped being a differentiator some time in 2024, and listing the tools I use tells you nothing about whether I can direct them.

The failures aren't in the code. They're on the seam between the text and the product.

Three from this site, all of them mine, all live for weeks before I noticed. The struck-out line is what the page claimed; underneath is what was true:

  • GRID_BREAKER · privacy policy “No network connections, servers, or cloud services.” The game ships Game Center leaderboards. The document was fluent, confident and wrong about my own app, sitting under a live App Store listing.
  • WristVault · landing page “Your collection never leaves your device.” The privacy page directly beneath it described iCloud sync. Both sentences were written to reassure and only one of them was accurate.
  • WristVault · call to action “Coming soon to the App Store” It was a href="#". The button did nothing, and it shipped.

None of these are bugs. They're claims that sounded right and weren't, in the one place only the person who built the thing can check. And I didn't catch them before shipping; I caught them in a review pass weeks later. That changed how I work: generated copy now gets the same treatment as generated code, and nothing goes out until it has been checked against what the product actually does, including the legal pages nobody reads.

The other half of the work is upstream. Rather than prompt my way through each app from scratch, I built a reusable project structure and a set of written standards that travel between them: what “good” means for this kind of work, written concretely enough to be followed. The tenth app starts where the ninth left off.

That transfers directly to working with a team, because it's the same skill. Writing down what “good” means precisely enough that someone else can act on it without you in the room is most of the job either way.

How I work

Every app here started with the same question: is this problem worth solving, who exactly has it, and what does a first version have to do before anyone will care? I look for something people already complain about, check whether the apps that exist actually fix it, then write down the smallest version worth downloading and hold that line while I build.

Scope is only part of it. I also set the price, write the store listing, sort out the translations and the compliance paperwork, and decide what the free version gives away. Those are product owner calls, and making every one of them myself is the quickest way I know to learn what each one costs.

What counts is how the app feels to use. Something that does less but needs no explaining beats something complete that doesn't.

Background

Ten years in e-commerce, four of them leading a team. I currently lead product for Gaming, Printers & Office at Coolblue, where I'm responsible for the strategy and commercial performance of those categories and coach six product managers and four product editors.

I've never held a product owner title. What I do have is a decade of being accountable for whether something actually worked commercially, and four apps where every product call was mine to make.
