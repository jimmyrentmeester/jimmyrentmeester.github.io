Jimmy Rentmeester

I turn ideas into finished, shipped products — usually on my own.

I design, build and release small iOS apps end to end: finding a problem worth solving, cutting the idea down to something shippable, writing it, and taking it all the way through the App Store — listing, pricing, localisation and the paperwork behind it.

Working alone means every trade-off is explicit. What goes in version one and what waits. What to charge and how. What to leave out to keep the thing understandable. Those decisions interest me at least as much as the code does.

BabyBeam

Baby monitor · iPhone & iPad

Turns two iPhones into a private video baby monitor. Audio and video go straight from phone to phone, end-to-end encrypted, and nothing is recorded or stored.

The call: every competitor routes video through a cloud account. Keeping the stream strictly between two devices meant giving up remote viewing — and became the entire reason to pick this one.

WristVault

Watch collection · iPhone & iPad

Catalogues a watch collection, logs what you wear, and exports an insurance-ready PDF. Everything stays on the device.

The call: pay once, never subscribe — in a category that has drifted to monthly fees. The free tier stays genuinely usable rather than crippled, so the paid unlock has to earn itself.

How I work

  • Ship it, then judge it A finished version one beats a perfect plan. Everything I have learned about these apps, I learned after release.
  • Cut until it is clear Most features are deferred, not rejected. A first version that explains itself is worth more than a complete one that does not.
  • Private by default No accounts, no ads, no tracking, in every app. It rules out easy growth tactics, which is precisely what makes it a promise.
  • Own the whole path Research, design, code, store listing, pricing, localisation, tax and compliance. Knowing what it takes to release changes what you decide to build.
